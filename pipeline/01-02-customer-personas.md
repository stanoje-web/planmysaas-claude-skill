# Stage 1.2 — Customer personas

Generate `./planmysaas-blueprint/01-02-customer-personas.md`. Read `01-01-problem-validation.md` first — every persona's pains must trace back to the problem statement in that file.

## Output structure

```markdown
# Customer personas — <Project name>

## Persona 1 — <Full name> (Primary)

**Role:** <job title / life role> · **Age:** <range> · **Income:** <bracket> · **Location:** <urban/suburban/rural, region type>

**A day in their life:** 3-4 sentences narrating a specific moment where the problem shows up — not a bio, a scene.

**Goals:**
- 3-4 concrete goals (what they're trying to achieve, not features they want)

**Frustrations:**
- 3-4 pains, each explicitly tied back to a line in the problem statement — don't invent new pains the problem validation didn't cover

**Current tools/workarounds:** What they use today, named specifically (app names, spreadsheets, a person they pay).

**Buying trigger:** The exact moment they start actively searching for a solution — an event, not a mood ("just got put in charge of X" not "feels overwhelmed").

**Objections:**
- 2-3 real reasons they wouldn't buy (price, trust, switching cost, "I'll just keep doing it manually")

**In their words:** One quote, written in first person, in their voice.

## Persona 2 — <Full name> (Secondary)
[same structure]

## Persona 3 — <Full name> (Secondary, optional)
[same structure]

## Comparison table

| Persona | Role | Primary pain | Buying trigger | Willingness to pay |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
```

## Quality rules

- Personas must be specific enough that two different readers would picture the same person — reject generic "busy professional" or "small business owner" descriptions with no concrete detail.
- Every frustration must map to something already stated in `01-01-problem-validation.md` — no orphan pains invented fresh here.
- Names must be full, ordinary names (not "Persona A" or "The Freelancer") — treat them as real people.
- The "day in their life" scene must include a specific trigger moment, not a mood description.
- Buying trigger must be an event a marketer could target ("renews their lease and reviews expenses" not "gets frustrated").
- Willingness to pay in the comparison table must be a number or tight range, not "high/medium/low."

## Tone

Plain English, written like character notes for a designer — concrete, human, no corporate persona-template jargon.
