# Stage 2.4 — Analogies & post-mortems

Generate `./planmysaas-blueprint/02-04-analogies-post-mortems.md`. Read `01-01-problem-validation.md` and `02-02-why-now.md` first — the analogies and post-mortems must be structurally similar to this specific problem and timing window, not generic startup lore.

## Output structure

```markdown
# Analogies & post-mortems — <Project name>

## How to read this file

One sentence: these are real, named companies chosen for structural similarity to this idea's situation (audience, wedge, or timing) — not for surface-level category match.

## Analogous successes (3-4)

For each:
- **Company** — real, named
- **Structural similarity** — what about their early situation matches this idea (narrow wedge, similar timing window, similar buyer type, similar bootstrapped-vs-funded path — be specific about which)
- **What worked** — the specific early move that mattered, not a generic "they had good product-market fit"
- **Transferable lesson for this idea** — one sentence, actionable, named back to this specific product

If no close match exists in this exact niche, say so explicitly and use the closest structurally-similar analogy from an adjacent space rather than inventing a company.

## Post-mortems (3-4)

For each:
- **Company** — real, named (well-documented failure or shutdown)
- **Structural similarity** — what about their situation matches a risk this idea faces
- **Specific, avoidable cause of failure** — the concrete mistake (not "ran out of money" alone — the decision that caused it: over-hired before PMF, picked a wedge too broad, monetization came too late, built for a market that couldn't pay, etc.)
- **What this idea should do differently** — one sentence, concrete, tied to a specific decision this founder will face

## Synthesis

**Patterns to repeat**
- 3-5 bullets, each a concrete behavior drawn from the successes above

**Patterns to avoid**
- 3-5 bullets, each a concrete behavior drawn from the post-mortems above
```

## Quality rules

- Every company named must be real — well-known startups, public post-mortems, or documented case studies. Never invent a company or blend two real companies into a fictional composite.
- If the niche is too obscure for an exact-match analogy, say so explicitly ("no direct match — closest structural analogy is X because Y") rather than forcing a weak fit or fabricating one.
- "What worked" and "cause of failure" must be specific decisions or events, not vague labels like "bad timing" or "good execution" with nothing under them.
- Each lesson must tie back to a decision this specific founder will actually face (pricing, wedge, channel, hiring pace) — not a generic startup platitude.
- Post-mortems should be picked for structural relevance to THIS idea's risks (e.g. same buyer type, same monetization challenge, same platform dependency), not just famous failures in general.

## Tone

Direct and concrete, like a founder-to-founder debrief. Name names, name the specific decision — no vague "lessons learned" language.
